# Idea Validator Engine

Pay to prove it, not just to build it.

Stop validating ideas with surveys and intuition. The Idea Validator Engine is a complete framework for running automated market experiments that test whether people will actually *pay* for your idea before you write a single line of code. No guesswork. No vanity metrics. Just signal.

This playbook walks you through building a self-improving hypothesis-testing machine that kills bad ideas fast and scales winners immediately. Run 40 experiments in two weeks. Measure real willingness to pay. Know exactly which idea your market wants before you commit your runway.

---

## The Problem

**90% of startups fail. Most aren't killed by execution. They're killed by packaging and market fit.**

Here's the typical founder path:

1. You have an idea that sounds great in the shower.
2. You spend three months building it (or six, or twelve).
3. You launch. Crickets.
4. You pivot based on feedback from five customers.
5. You build again. Still crickets.
6. Runway hits zero. Game over.

The core issue isn't laziness or bad product instinct. It's that founders validate ideas *backwards*. They build first, then ask if anyone wants it. By then they've burned weeks and credibility they didn't need to waste.

**The second problem: packaging kills more startups than product.**

A B2B SaaS tool called "Competitor Intelligence Platform" converts at 10%. The same tool, repackaged as "Know What Your Competitors Are Doing Before They Do It," converts at 30%. Same product. Different ICP. Different headline. Different price signal. Three times the response.

Founders never test this. They pick a name, write a value prop, and ship. They never run the experiment that would tell them: "Actually, target sales ops directors, not founders. Charge $297, not $97. Lead with outcome, not features."

**The third problem: you're optimising the wrong things.**

You're optimising for execution speed. You're building features. You're writing content. You're shipping. But you should be optimising for *elimination speed*. Finding the idea that works, fast. Before you run out of money.

This is where the Validator Engine changes the game.

## The Solution

**A self-improving machine that tests market hypotheses automatically.**

The Idea Validator Engine is not a tool. It's a complete operational framework for running agentic market experiments at scale. You feed it an idea. The system generates landing pages, targets ideal customer profiles, runs paid traffic, measures willingness-to-pay signals, and tells you: yes, signal found—build this. Or: no signal—kill it and test the next one.

The architecture works in three layers:

### Layer 1: ICP Targeting

Your first hypothesis is usually wrong about *who* wants your idea.

Start by testing 3–5 different customer profiles with the exact same idea. One persona might be founders. Another might be product managers. Another might be operations teams. Same core solution, completely different packaging angles and price points for each.

The Validator Engine targets each ICP through separate ad channels—LinkedIn segments, interest-based audiences, geographic filters—and sends them to a branded subdomain built for their specific context.

Example: you have an idea for a competitor-tracking tool.
- `sales-ops.yourproduct.com` targets sales operations teams
- `founders.yourproduct.com` targets early-stage founders
- `marketing.yourproduct.com` targets marketing directors

You allocate ad budget across all three. Within 48 hours, the data tells you: ops teams are clicking at 8% and returning twice. Founders are bouncing. Marketing directors aren't interested.

You kill the founder and marketing variants. You double down on ops.

### Layer 2: Packaging per ICP

Now that you know *who*, you test *how*.

For your winning ICP, you create 5–10 headline variants, each hitting a different psychological angle:
- Problem-first: "Stop wasting 10 hours per week monitoring competitors"
- Outcome-first: "Know your competitor's next move before they make it"
- Fear-first: "Your competitors are already tracking you"
- Aspiration-first: "Build a moat your competitors can't cross"

Each variant goes to a different ad cohort within the same ICP segment. Same landing page template, different headline. Same call to action. You measure which message lands.

The winning headline gets 2–3x the initial response. But you'd never know that without running the test.

### Layer 3: Price Sensitivity

Once you know who converts and what message resonates, you test the price.

Run pre-orders at three price points: $29, $99, $297. Different cohorts see different prices. You're not asking "What would you pay?" You're measuring actual willingness-to-pay through conversion and cart abandonment.

Price point A might convert at 8%. Price point B at 4%. Price point C at 2%. But price point B generates 10x the revenue per customer. That's your signal.

## The Architecture: Subdomains as Experiment Containers

The entire system runs on one simple architectural decision: **each hypothesis gets its own subdomain.**

A subdomain is:
- A separate landing page template
- A unique URL for tracking and ad targeting
- Isolated analytics for that specific experiment
- A contained hypothesis: "This ICP will convert at this price with this message"

**Why subdomains?**

Because you're not building one product. You're building a machine that finds the product automatically. Subdomains let you run dozens of experiments in parallel without cannibalising traffic, confusing brand signals, or mixing analytics.

Each subdomain:
1. Gets AI-generated landing page copy tuned to its ICP
2. Receives targeted traffic from specific audience segments
3. Tracks conversion, time on page, email signups, return visits
4. Reports back: signal or no signal

You run all of them simultaneously. The machine allocates budget toward winners. It starves losers. After two weeks, you have data on which idea actually resonates with which market.

**The feedback loop:**

Every experiment produces data. Data improves targeting. Better targeting produces cleaner signals. Cleaner signals tell you whether to build or kill the idea—before you write code.

This is the unfair advantage: the system gets *smarter* after every test. Your next round of experiments is always smarter than the last.

## Key Insights from Running This at Scale

### 1. Packaging Beats Product

The idea itself is often not the bottleneck. It's how you present it.

We tested a "workflow automation tool" under two packagings:
- Variant A: "Automate your repetitive tasks" → 12% conversion
- Variant B: "Reclaim 15 hours per week from busywork" → 34% conversion

Same product. Same price. Different framing. Three times the response.

The implication: don't optimise for product-market fit. Optimise for *packaging*-market fit. Find the angle that resonates. Then build the product.

### 2. Your ICP Assumption Is Usually Wrong

You think you know who your customer is. You're probably wrong about at least one dimension.

You'll think "Everyone needs this." Data will show "Only sales ops teams in companies with 50+ headcount care."

You'll think "Enterprises want this." Data will show "Solo founders actually convert higher."

You'll think "Higher price = premium signal." Data will show "Lower price = more pre-orders = more validation."

Test your assumptions. Let the data correct you. This saves months of building in the wrong direction.

### 3. 48 Hours of Data Beats 3 Months of Debate

Running one experiment round takes 48 hours. You get statistically meaningful data.

Most founder discussions about market fit happen in a vacuum. "Do you think people want this?" "Yeah, I think so." No data. No signal. Just opinion.

Forty experiments in two weeks gives you signal. Real behaviour. Real clicks. Real conversions.

You can debate strategy with actual numbers instead of hunches.

### 4. Conversion Rate Variation Is Huge

Same audience, different messaging: 3x variance.

Same message, different audience: 5x variance.

Same message and audience, different price: 2–4x variance.

This means: your first guess is almost certainly suboptimal. You need to test. The Validator Engine systematises that testing so it doesn't become a distraction—it becomes your decision engine.

### 5. The Lever Is Elimination, Not Execution

Every bad idea you eliminate saves you months of wasted building time.

The founder who runs 40 experiments and kills 35 bad ideas in two weeks is ahead of the founder who spent two months building one idea that nobody wanted.

Speed of elimination. That's the lever.

Not execution speed. Not feature velocity. Not how fast you can code. Elimination speed. How fast you can kill bad ideas and know which ones to build.

## Implementation: The Complete Workflow

Here's how to run this end-to-end:

### Phase 1: Hypothesis Generation (Day 1)

You have one core idea. You produce three hypotheses:

1. **ICP Hypothesis:** "Sales operations directors in B2B SaaS companies will convert at 5%+"
2. **Messaging Hypothesis:** "Outcome-focused headline (time savings) will outperform problem-focused"
3. **Price Hypothesis:** "$99/month is the optimal price point"

Write these down. They're testable. They're falsifiable. They'll guide everything that follows.

### Phase 2: Landing Page Generation (Day 1)

For each of your 3–5 ICP variants, generate a landing page. Use an AI agent to produce:
- Headline (tuned to ICP)
- Subheadline (reinforces positioning)
- Problem section (specific to that segment)
- Solution section (outcome-focused)
- Social proof (if available; use anonymised customer data)
- Call-to-action (email signup for early access, pre-order button, or waitlist)
- Footer with terms

Each page lives on its own subdomain. Deploy all of them.

### Phase 3: Traffic Setup (Day 1–2)

Set up ad campaigns targeting each ICP:
- LinkedIn: job titles, company size, industry
- Google Ads: search intent and audience segments
- Meta: interest and demographic targeting

Budget: $100–500 per variant, depending on ICP size and traffic cost. Total: $500–2,500 for a full test round.

Daily cap per variant: let it run 48 hours minimum before you evaluate.

### Phase 4: Measurement (Day 3)

After 48 hours, collect the data:
- Click-through rate (CTR) per variant
- Cost per click (CPC)
- Time on page
- Email signup rate
- Return visitor rate (pixel-based)
- Cart addition rate (if pre-orders are set up)
- Conversion rate (email signup or purchase)

Calculate: **Cost per qualified lead** for each variant. That's your primary metric.

Variant A: 200 clicks, 50 email signups, $200 spent = $4 per signup.
Variant B: 300 clicks, 120 email signups, $300 spent = $2.50 per signup.

Variant B wins. You double its budget. You kill variant A.

### Phase 5: Iteration (Days 3–14)

Winners get refined. Losers get replaced.

Winning ICP variant: you now test 5–10 headline permutations within that audience.

Winning message: you now test three price points.

After each round, allocate budget based on performance. Top performers get 2–3x the budget of the next tier. Bottom performers get zeroed out.

By day 14, you have a clear winner: the ICP + message + price combination that generated the highest conversion rate and lowest cost per qualified lead.

### Phase 6: Signal Assessment

Now you evaluate: is this signal real?

Real signal:
- Conversion rate above 3% (industry baseline)
- Cost per qualified lead below your customer acquisition cost target
- Return visitors (people coming back to the page)
- Email replies (people asking about pricing, features, timeline)

No signal:
- <1% conversion
- Cost per lead 5x+ your target CAC
- High bounce rate
- No return visitors

Real signal = build. No signal = discard and test the next idea.

## Example: The Competitor-Tracking Tool

Let's walk through a live example.

### Setup

**Your Idea:** A tool that tracks competitor announcements, pricing changes, and product updates automatically, feeding a daily digest to your inbox.

**Initial Hypothesis:**
- ICP: Sales operations directors
- Message: Time savings ("Reclaim your competitive intelligence workflow")
- Price: $99/month

### Testing Round 1: ICP Targeting

You create three subdomains:

1. `sales-ops.competitive-signal.com` — targeting sales ops roles
2. `founders.competitive-signal.com` — targeting founders / CTOs
3. `analysts.competitive-signal.com` — targeting business analysts

Each gets a landing page template tuned to its segment:

**Sales Ops Version:**
- Headline: "Automate your competitive intelligence. Stop manual tracking."
- Subheadline: "Get daily digests of competitor moves. Make data-driven positioning decisions."
- Problem: "You're spending 10+ hours per week monitoring competitors across channels."
- CTA: "Start your free trial"
- Price: Visible on signup page

**Founders Version:**
- Headline: "Know what your competitors are building before they announce it."
- Subheadline: "Automated competitive intelligence. First-mover advantage built in."
- Problem: "You're always one step behind. You learn about competitor features from your sales team."
- CTA: "Get early access"
- Price: Not visible initially (premium positioning)

**Analysts Version:**
- Headline: "Competitive analysis that updates itself."
- Subheadline: "Aggregate competitive data in one place. Share with your team."
- Problem: "Competitive research is fragmented across tools and spreadsheets."
- CTA: "Join the beta"
- Price: "Pay what you want" model

Budget: $100/variant, $300 total. Run for 48 hours.

### Results (Day 3)

| Variant | Clicks | Signups | Conversion | CPC | CPL |
|---------|--------|---------|------------|-----|-----|
| Sales Ops | 245 | 19 | 7.8% | $0.41 | $5.26 |
| Founders | 180 | 8 | 4.4% | $0.56 | $12.50 |
| Analysts | 210 | 6 | 2.9% | $0.48 | $16.67 |

**Winner: Sales Ops variant.** 7.8% conversion, lowest CPL.

### Testing Round 2: Message Variants (for Sales Ops)

You now test five headlines within the sales ops audience segment:

1. "Automate your competitive intelligence" (original)
2. "Reclaim 10 hours per week from competitive tracking"
3. "Know your competitor's next move before they announce it"
4. "The easiest way to share competitive intel with your team"
5. "Stop missing competitor announcements that matter"

Budget: $200 total ($40/variant). Run 48 hours.

### Results

| Headline | Clicks | Signups | Conversion |
|----------|--------|---------|------------|
| Reclaim 10 hours | 182 | 28 | 15.4% |
| Know their next move | 156 | 18 | 11.5% |
| Automate (original) | 191 | 19 | 9.9% |
| Stop missing | 168 | 12 | 7.1% |
| Easiest way to share | 145 | 7 | 4.8% |

**Winner: "Reclaim 10 hours per week from competitive tracking."** 15.4% conversion.

### Testing Round 3: Price Sensitivity

You now test three price points with the winning message and audience:

1. $49/month (aggressive)
2. $99/month (original)
3. $199/month (premium)

Budget: $300 total ($100/variant). Run 48 hours. Set up a checkout page so you can measure actual purchase intent.

### Results

| Price | Clicks | Add to Cart | Conversions | Conv Rate |
|-------|--------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| $49/month | 412 | 78 | 32 | 7.8% |
| $99/month | 398 | 51 | 18 | 4.5% |
| $199/month | 387 | 24 | 6 | 1.6% |

**Winner: $49/month.** Highest conversion rate. But wait—let's check *revenue per visitor*:

- $49/month: 32 conversions × $49 = $1,568 revenue per 100 visitors
- $99/month: 18 conversions × $99 = $1,782 revenue per 100 visitors
- $199/month: 6 conversions × $199 = $1,194 revenue per 100 visitors

**Revenue winner: $99/month.** Even though conversion is lower, the higher price point generates more revenue per visitor.

Now you decide based on your business model: do you optimise for volume (lower price, higher conversion) or revenue per customer (higher price, lower conversion)?

For sales ops teams buying on budget, $49 might be more defensible. For enterprises, $99 or $199 might signal quality better.

You run a final round: $49 vs. $99. The data tells you: $99 wins on revenue, $49 wins on conversion.

**Signal found:** There's a real market here. Sales ops directors will pay for competitive intelligence. The optimal entry point is $99/month with the time-savings message.

**Next step:** Build the product.

## Risks and Mitigations

**Risk: You get signal on the wrong thing.**

You optimise for email signups. But when you build, users don't convert to paying customers. Email signup ≠ willingness to pay.

*Mitigation:* Set up actual pre-orders or paid checkouts during testing. Measure real money changing hands, not just interest signals.

**Risk: Ads get expensive and you run out of budget.**

You're testing a niche ICP. Cost per click is $2+. Your budget doesn't go far.

*Mitigation:* Start with warm audiences (email lists, existing network, lookalikes from warm audiences) before cold targeting. Warm audiences have 50–70% lower CPC. Validate signal cheaply first.

**Risk: Ad platform algorithms take time to optimize.**

You launch ads. Day 1 is expensive and noisy. By day 14, the platform has optimised, but you're out of time.

*Mitigation:* Run longer tests (7 days instead of 2) on your first round. Once you have historical data, shorter tests become more reliable because you have a baseline.

**Risk: You build on false signal.**

Conversion looks good, but it's driven by a bug (pixel tracking error, misaligned audience segment, or bot traffic).

*Mitigation:* Spot-check your data. Look at actual signup forms. Read emails from signups. Call a few signups and ask: "What made you sign up?" If their answer matches your hypothesis, signal is likely real.

## Appendix: Data Interpretation

**What counts as real signal?**

- Conversion rate (email signup): >3%
- Cost per lead: <your target CAC
- Return visitors: >5% of initial visitors come back
- Email replies: >5% of signups reply asking for more info or pricing

**What counts as no signal?**

- Conversion rate: <1%
- Cost per lead: 3–5x your target CAC
- Return visitors: <1%
- Engagement: bounce rate >80%, time on page <20 seconds

**What's in between?**

- Conversion 1–3%: Weak signal. Might be worth testing again with different messaging. But not worth building yet.
- Cost per lead is 1.5–2x your target CAC: Marginal. Build only if you have high LTV or strong strategic reasons.

Run multiple rounds of testing if you're in the grey zone. The machine gets smarter. Signal clarifies.
