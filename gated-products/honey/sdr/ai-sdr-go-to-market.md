# AI SDR Go-to-Market Plan

A complete, phase-by-phase blueprint for launching an AI sales agent product to profitability. This playbook covers Foundation, Beta, Scale, and Expansion phases, with pricing strategy, customer acquisition channels, operational KPIs, and risk mitigation tactics. Use this to compress 12 months of learning into 12 weeks.

## The Problem

You've built an AI sales agent that actually works. It can prospect, write personalised emails, engage on social media, and qualify leads without a human touch. Now you need to sell it. Most founders get this part wrong—they either launch at premium pricing with no social proof, burn through money on paid acquisition before proving product-market fit, or launch on every channel simultaneously and burn out.

The real issue: SDR products live in a brutally competitive space. Buyers are sceptical of automation claims. They've been burned by lead generation tools before. And they're fragmented—bootstrapped founders care about margin, seed-stage startups care about lead volume, enterprise cares about customisation and integration. A one-size-fits-all launch fails.

What actually works is a compressed, evidence-first go-to-market that de-risks each phase before moving to the next. Start small with free users who will give you honest feedback. Use that feedback to build pricing confidence. Then scale acquisition in channels where the message resonates. Finally, build a moat by adding features competitors can't easily copy.

## The Framework: 4-Phase Launch

### Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1–4)
**Goal:** Validate core use case with free beta cohort. Prove the product works before charging.

**Why this matters:** Most founders skip this and go straight to a pricing page. They guess what features matter. They set the wrong tone. By Week 4, they've launched, nobody signed up, and they blame the market instead of the messaging. Wrong. The market is fine—your positioning is unclear.

**What to do:**
- Launch with 1–2 channels only. Email + LinkedIn + one social channel (X or LinkedIn). Video generation is v1.1, not v1.0.
- Target 10 beta users. Not 100. You need to talk to every single one weekly, watch them use the product, fix the critical bugs.
- Price: Free. No freemium trap, no 14-day trial. Free. You need to prove stickiness before money enters the conversation.
- Update publicly once per week: metrics, bugs fixed, features shipped. This builds urgency in the waitlist and forces you to move fast.

**Key insight:** The best feedback comes from users who have something to lose if your product fails. Free users are volunteers. Pay them with transparency and urgency.

**Success threshold:** 10 active daily users, >50% weekly login rate, at least 5 pieces of user-driven feature feedback.

### Phase 2: Beta to Paid (Weeks 5–8)
**Goal:** Introduce pricing tiers and convert 50 free users to paid. Prove willingness to pay exists.

**Why this matters:** This is where most AI tools die. Founders either underprice ($9/month and chase volume) or overprice ($299/month and get zero conversions). You need proof that *some* customer segment will pay *more* than $49/month and stay for >3 months.

**Pricing structure:**
| Tier | Price | Features | ICP |
|------|-------|----------|-----|
| **Starter** | $49/mo | Email + LinkedIn outreach, 500 leads/month, basic templates | Bootstrapped founders, solo founders |
| **Growth** | $149/mo | All channels (email, LinkedIn, X, Reddit), 2,500 leads/month, custom templates, Slack notifications | Seed-stage startups (5–15 people) |
| **Pro** | $299/mo | Everything + video generation, 10,000 leads/month, AI-powered follow-up sequencing, dedicated onboarding | Scale-ups (Series A, 15+ people) |
| **Enterprise** | $499+/mo | Unlimited leads, dedicated account manager, custom integrations, white-label option | Enterprise, agencies |

**Why these tiers work:** Each tier is a distinct customer profile with distinct needs and willingness to pay. Starter is for cash-conscious builders. Growth is for teams that have found product-market fit and need volume. Pro is for teams that want competitive moat (video is hard to copy). Enterprise is for organisations that need customisation or white-label.

**Positioning each tier:**
- **Most Popular badge on Growth.** Growth is your anchor. It's where most revenue will come from. Make it obvious that scaling founders choose this tier.
- **Starter is aspirational.** "Start here, grow with us to Growth."
- **Pro is for the impatient.** Video generation is a moat. Differentiate hard here.

**What to do:**
- Run a private beta launch with your 10 Week 1 users. Offer them Growth tier for free for 2 months ("founding user discount"). Record their objections. Do they think email outreach is too commodified? Do they want more channels? Do they want integration with their CRM?
- Open a waitlist with a simple landing page. Feature comparison table, pricing, "Most Popular" badge, 14-day free trial offer for the first 50 signups.
- Launch on Product Hunt. This is peak attention. You have one shot per lifetime. Don't waste it. Get feedback from maker community. Expect 50–100 upvotes if you're top 10 of the day.
- Commit to shipping one feature per week based on feedback. Users notice momentum. Momentum builds trust. Trust converts to paid.
- Start cold outreach. Manually email 50 ideal customer profiles (ICP). You're targeting teams of 5–15 people who've been hiring, growing, and need leads. Find them on Twitter, pull their email from their website, send a personal note saying you built this for people like them and want their feedback.

**Why cold outreach matters:** It sounds backwards to do this during beta, but it's not. You'll get replies from founders who *actually need* what you built, not vanity signups. Their feedback is gold. Some will convert to your first paid users—not because your product is perfect, but because you showed up.

**Success threshold:** 50 paid users across tiers, 70%+ trial-to-paid conversion rate for Growth tier, <10% churn month 1, at least 3 case studies or user testimonials.

### Phase 3: Scale (Weeks 9–12)
**Goal:** Expand to all acquisition channels, add key differentiating feature (video generation), hit 200 paying customers and $20K MRR.

**Why this matters:** By now you've proven product-market fit exists. You know which customer segment pays and stays. You know what the core value prop is. Now you can spend money acquiring customers without gambling. This phase is about compressing growth via multi-channel acquisition and feature expansion.

**Customer acquisition channels:**

| Channel | Tactic | When | ROI |
|---------|--------|------|-----|
| **Twitter/X** | Build in public: weekly metrics threads, customer wins, benchmarks, "why we built this" threads. Retweet customer successes. | Ongoing | High (if authentic) |
| **LinkedIn** | Founder posts about sales automation trends, case studies, objection handling. Founder profile is your brand. | Ongoing | High (if you have 5K+ followers) |
| **Product Hunt** | Already done Week 6, but revisit: run a promotion week, respond to every comment, gather testimonials. | Week 9 | Medium (diminishing) |
| **Cold email** | Scale your Week 7 playbook. Segment ICPs: e-commerce, SaaS, recruitment, real estate. Write segment-specific emails. Aim for 2% reply rate. | Ongoing | Medium ($50–100 CAC) |
| **Communities** | r/saas, r/startups, r/growth, indie hacker forums. No spam. Answer questions. Mention product when relevant. | Ongoing | Medium |
| **Affiliate/partnerships** | Partner with sales coaches, CRM reviewers, podcasters. Offer 20–30% commission on first month. | Week 10+ | High (if partner audience aligns) |
| **Webinars/workshops** | Host free 30-min workshops: "How to automate your first sales process", "Cold email mistakes to avoid". Gate behind email signup. | Weeks 11–12 | Medium |

**Why channel stack matters:** No single channel will get you to 200 customers. Twitter gets you brand awareness. Cold email gets you your first 50. Communities and partnerships get you your next 50. By Week 12, you're not dependent on any one channel—you've built a portfolio.

**Feature expansion: Video generation**
- Most critical Week 9–12 feature. Why? Video personalisation is the highest-engagement sales tactic. AI video generation de-risks it: founders can send 100 personalised videos/month without hiring someone. This is a moat.
- Ship a basic version: template + face swap + minimal customisation. Don't build a video editor. Just enable the core use case: "I want to send personalised video to 50 cold leads".
- Position it as "Pro tier exclusive" to justify the $299/mo price.

**What to do:**
- Week 9: Ship video generation. Announce it everywhere (Twitter, Product Hunt update, email list, community forums).
- Week 9–10: Scale cold email. Create 3–5 segment-specific email templates. Measure reply rate, reply-to-paid conversion. Allocate budget towards highest-ROI segments.
- Week 10–11: Launch affiliate program. Find 5 partners: sales coaches, CRM reviewers, podcast hosts in the SaaS space. Offer 30% commission on first month.
- Week 11–12: Run two webinars. Goal: 200–300 registrations each, 20–30% attendance, 5–10% conversion to paid.
- Ongoing: Post twice per week on Twitter about metrics, customer wins, benchmarks. Retweet customer content. Build a community.

**Success threshold:** 200 paying customers, $20K MRR, CAC <$100, <5% monthly churn, NPS >50, 3+ feature requests from customers that you're shipping.

### Phase 4: Expansion (Month 4–6)
**Goal:** Build moat, expand TAM, hit $100K MRR and 500+ customers. Add human layer (white-label or managed service option).

**Why this matters:** At $20K MRR, you're sustainable but not yet a business. Competitors will enter. Margins will compress. You need a moat: a capability that's hard to copy. The moat here is video + human blending: offer a managed service tier where you run outreach campaigns for customers. This keeps them sticky and opens a higher-price tier.

**Building the moat:**
- **Managed service tier ($1,500–5,000/mo).** You run campaigns for them. Your SDR AI does the heavy lifting. Your humans (hire 1–2 contractors) do the final personalisation and validation. This generates 10x margin vs software-only.
- **White-label tier ($5,000+/mo).** Agencies want to rebrand your product and sell to their clients. Let them. You get enterprise pricing and they get a competitive offering.
- **Integrations.** Connect to the tools they already use: CRM, email, Slack, HubSpot. Each integration increases switching costs and stickiness.

**What to do:**
- Month 4: Hire your first non-founder. This could be a customer success person (to reduce churn and upsell Growth → Pro), or a contractor to run managed service campaigns.
- Month 4–5: Build the top 3 integrations. Prioritise by demand. If customers keep asking for Salesforce integration, build it. Use Zapier as a stopgap if you're not ready for native.
- Month 5: Launch managed service tier. Start with 3–5 customers at $2,000/mo. Get feedback. Refine the playbook.
- Month 5–6: Launch white-label tier. Find 2–3 agencies to beta this. They'll tell you what's missing.
- Month 6: Fundraise or plan for profitability. At $100K MRR, you have options: take small seed round and hire 2–3 people, or stay profitable and bootstrap to $500K ARR.

**Success threshold:** $100K MRR, 500+ customers, <4% monthly churn, NPS >55, 1 managed service customer, 1 white-label partner signed.

## What Most Founders Get Wrong

**1. Pricing too conservatively.** Founders say "I'll launch at $29/mo to get volume." Then they hit the ceiling: they need 1,000+ customers at $29 to reach $29K MRR. That requires enterprise-grade marketing. Instead, start at $49–149/mo. Better to have 100 customers at $149/mo ($14.9K MRR) than 1,000 at $15/mo ($15K MRR with 10x support load).

**2. Too many channels too early.** You're not Amazon. You don't have 10 CAC buckets. Pick 2 channels. Get 50 customers. Understand the unit economics. Then add a third. Spreading yourself across 10 channels week 1 guarantees you'll do none of them well.

**3. Skipping the foundation phase.** "I just want to launch and iterate." You'll iterate on the wrong assumptions. Spend 4 weeks validating with free users first. It compresses 6 months of learning into 4 weeks.

**4. Building enterprise features in v1.** White-label, advanced integrations, dedicated support—these are Phase 4. In Phase 1, build the core SDR loop: prospecting, personalised outreach, follow-up. Everything else is distraction.

**5. Not building in public.** Silence kills momentum. By Week 3, you feel like you've been working forever with no traction. If you're posting weekly metrics, you get feedback, you attract users, and you stay motivated.

## Metrics to Watch

**Phase 1 (Foundation):**
- Daily active users: Target 7+ (70% of 10 beta users)
- Weekly retention: Target >50%
- Feature requests per user: Target 2–3 (tells you what matters)

**Phase 2 (Beta to Paid):**
- Trial starts: Target 50+
- Trial-to-paid conversion: Target 70% (indicates strong product-market fit)
- MRR: Target $2–5K (50 users × $49–149 blended ARPU)
- Churn: Target <10% (you're still iterating)
- CAC: Target <$50 (word-of-mouth + organic should dominate)

**Phase 3 (Scale):**
- Paid users: Target 200+
- MRR: Target $20K
- CAC: Target $50–100 (you're now buying ads/partnerships)
- Churn: Target <5%
- NPS: Target >50 (strong product-market fit)

**Phase 4 (Expansion):**
- Paid users: Target 500+
- MRR: Target $100K
- CAC: Target <$100 (mix of organic + paid)
- Churn: Target <4%
- NPS: Target >55

## Risk Register & Mitigations

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| Low signup conversion | Medium | High | A/B test landing page messaging. Test 3 value props: "Save 10 hours/week", "Send 50+ personalised emails/day", "Automate lead qualification". Measure CTR and conversion for each. |
| High churn (>10% monthly) | Medium | High | Run cohort analysis Week 8. If users active Day 1 but inactive Day 7, your onboarding is broken. If active Day 7 but inactive Day 30, your product isn't sticky. Fix accordingly. |
| Competitors enter and undercut pricing | High | Medium | Your moat is video + human layer (Phase 4). Don't compete on price—compete on outcomes. Focus on case studies, benchmarks, customer testimonials. Show that your customers get 3x more meetings than competitors' customers. |
| Technical issues (API downtime, false positives) | Medium | High | Have a manual fallback. If your API is down, users should still be able to send emails manually. Offer 2-hour SLA support for Pro+ tiers. Log every failure and ship fixes weekly. |
| Acquisition cost too high (>$150/user) | Medium | High | This means your messaging is off or channels are wrong. Diagnostic: which channel has lowest CAC? Double down on it. Which has highest CAC? Kill it. Iterate on messaging based on feedback from lowest-CAC channel. |
| Founder burnout | High | Medium | Hire ops person by Month 3. Automate support via chatbot. Build a sales script so you're not writing 50 custom emails/day. Cap your email load to 10/day. |

## How to Use This Playbook

**Week 1:** Read this end-to-end. Customise the pricing table for your product. Identify 2 customer segments you'll target in Phase 1. Start building a list of 20 potential beta users.

**Week 2–3:** Finish product, set up analytics (Mixpanel, Amplitude, or Segment), configure email tracking. Ship beta to 10 users. Start cold outreach to 20 ICPs.

**Week 4–5:** Run user interviews with all 10 beta users. Document feedback. Update product roadmap. Plan Weeks 5–8.

**Week 6–8:** Build landing page, set up pricing, run Product Hunt launch, close first 50 paying users. Update metrics dashboard every Friday. Post metrics on Twitter.

**Week 9–12:** Scale channels, ship video feature, hit 200 customers, $20K MRR. Plan Phase 4: managed service and integrations.

**Post-launch:** Review this playbook quarterly. Which phases did you nail? Which did you skip? Capture learnings and add a section: "What Worked for Our Product" so the next person moving faster has compressed learning.

## Final Thought

The most common failure mode is "I launched and nobody came." In 90% of cases, it's not because your product is bad. It's because:
- You launched all at once with zero social proof
- You charged too much or too little with no confidence
- You picked the wrong channels and marketing message
- You went dark and lost momentum

This playbook compresses a year of SaaS founder learning into 12 weeks. Use it. Adapt it. Ship it. And document what happens so the next founder doesn't have to re-learn from scratch.
